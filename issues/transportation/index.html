Transportation

Too Fast Too Furious

Transportation is a problem.

Transportation as an industry is the fastest growing energy end-use industry contributing to greenhouse gas emissions. On the global scale, transportation accounts for about 15% of the total greenhouse gas emissions and 23% of the carbon dioxide emissions. A common belief is that the majority of those emissions come from commercial processes such as airplanes and ocean liners, but in reality, land transportation—specifically passenger cars control a dominating 80% of total transportation energy use. With such rapidly growing countries and cities, the resulting urban sprawl can make many modes of public transportation ineffective and convince citizens to buy their own transportation. With growing economies and a generalized increase in income and economic capacity, people are able and willing to spend extra money on individualized transportation and between the turn of the century and 2050, the number of motor-vehicles is projected to triple which will have an absolutely destructive impact on the climate as well as unmarked effects on the health of the citizens of the world.

But there is hope, most transportation mitigation occurs through technological advancements and change in public behavioral change. The biggest change recently is, of course, electric vehicles and their increasing popularity, but there are a number of smaller steps each person can take to mitigate their own transportation and its effect on the environment. Simple behavior changes like riding the public bus or the subway more have a big effect, especially in cities where the transportation runs on electric rails or power grids and the city itself receives relatively clean electricity (from hydro dams, solar grids, wind farms, etc.) An even easier way is the purchasing of personal transport such as a bike or a scooter that doesn’t use fuel (or only electricity) at all.
